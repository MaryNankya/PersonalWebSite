    • RESEARCH

      To achieve the goals of the fifth generation (5G) network, it is highly desirable to improve the area throughput by increasing the spectral efficiency rather than by increasing bandwidth or cell density. Massive MIMO (Multiple Input Multiple Output) is a technique which increases the spectral efficiency of cellular networks by deploying hundreds or thousands of antennas at base stations and performing coherent transceiver processing. This methodâ€™s ability to multiply the capacity of the antenna links has made it an essential element of wireless standards including 802.11n (Wi-Fi), 802.11ac (Wi-Fi), HSPA+, WiMAX and LTE. Deploying a system with hundreds of antennas and terminals requires complex signal processing, channel estimation, and several other techniques. Some of the other challenges in this system are reducing pilot contamination and power consumption, making low-cost antenna components and finding an extra degree of freedom.

    • My current research project includes three primary aims:
    • 1. to develop and design algorithms and hardware architectures that will decrease the complexity associated with currently existing precoding, detection, and channel estimation algorithm and increase the spectral efficiency.
    • 2. to find new deployment scenarios for massive MIMO system and to find innovative training schemes to reduce pilot contamination problem.
    • 3. to reduce internal power consumption to get higher energy efficiency.
    • This proposed technique can be utilized in achieving the goals of 5G communication networks by small, medium and large corporations and this will also help in implementing IoT and machine to machine communication.
